Decentralized Identifiers (DIDs) are a new type of identifier that enables verifiable, decentralized digital identity. Unlike traditional identifiers (email addresses, usernames, phone numbers), DIDs are created, owned, and controlled by the identity subject — without requiring permission from any centralized registry or authority.

## Why DIDs Matter

Traditional identity systems have a fundamental problem: **they depend on centralized authorities**. Your email is controlled by Google or Microsoft. Your social identity lives on Meta's servers. Your government ID is issued and revoked by the state.

DIDs flip this model. A DID is:

- **Self-owned**: You create it, you control it
- **Persistent**: It doesn't depend on any single organization's continued existence
- **Resolvable**: Anyone can look up the public keys and service endpoints associated with a DID
- **Cryptographically verifiable**: Ownership is proven through digital signatures, not passwords

## Anatomy of a DID

A DID looks like this:

```
did:web:example.com:users:alice
```

It has three parts:
1. **Scheme**: Always `did:`
2. **Method**: Specifies how the DID is created and resolved (e.g., `web`, `key`, `peer`)
3. **Method-specific identifier**: Unique within the method's namespace

## DID Methods in Practice

| Method | Resolution | Use Case |
|--------|-----------|----------|
| `did:web` | HTTPS/DNS | Organizations, public entities |
| `did:key` | Self-contained | Ephemeral, quick setup |
| `did:peer` | Peer-to-peer | Private pairwise connections |

Each method makes different trade-offs between decentralization, ease of use, and security guarantees.
